KEYNOTE ADDRESS
“ACCELERATING A SUSTAINABLE TRANSITION TO AVERT A +2°C world”


Dr. Winston Chow

Associate Professor of Science, Technology and Society,
College of Integrative Studies,
Singapore Management University

The recent news from a series of Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) reports paints a gloomy picture of a world affected by climate disruption, with increasing risks driven by compounding and cascading climate hazards across natural and human systems, and difficult challenges in addressing the root causes of climate change. Even though the world is slowly – but surely – taking belated action to stop the climate emergency, much more tangible work is needed lest we end up with more paralysing “blah, blah, blahs” as Greta Thunberg notes; the outcome of which likely leads to a warming world well in excess of the Paris warming limits of 2 °C with disastrous consequences to the most vulnerable natural ecosystems and human populations. In this keynote, the speaker intends to focus on one blueprint to avert this outcome – that of climate resilient development – which leads to outcomes in which a sustainable transition of human systems occurs while keeping to warming to 1.5 °C. He will elaborate on what does such a development entail (e.g. on adaptation to climate risk, mitigation of climate emissions through behavioural and infrastructural changes, and a focus on sustainable development); on why it is needed urgently and extensively (e.g. how ad hoc approaches that do not include but exclude stakeholders that lead to maladaptation and mitigation failures from unintended consequences); and conclude by stressing that climate resilient development requires an inclusive approach in which youth delegates have an outsized role in both enabling and maintaining the sustainability impacts in the years to come. Of Doughnuts and Doldrums: the Built Environment and Climate Change Mr. Szue Hann Tan

Chairman of Sustainability,
Singapore Institute of Architects

The built environment accounts for about 40% of the world’s energy consumption – and can be attributed as a major contributor to climate change. This talk looks at the best modes of architectural practice and sustainable development in mitigating climate change, turning buildings into agents of recovery, while respecting the social foundation and ecological ceiling limits posited by the model of Doughnut Economics. Referencing the Singapore Institute of Architects’ 7 EDGs (Environmental Design Guidelines), themselves adapted from the UN SDGs, the talk looks into a future where the built environment becomes a solution for, rather than a contributor to, climate change. Synopsis ›

ACCELERATING CHANGE PANEL
“FROM COMMITMENT TO ACTION: GRASSROOT INITIATIVES IN CLIMATE ACTION IN ASIA”


‘Do not go gentle into that good night’ - The Fight for a Sustainable Future Ms. Cassandra Yip

Founder & CEO,
Earth School Singapore

The power of simple ground-up environmental initiatives driven by local eco-communities is often overlooked in favour of complex, scientific "smart solutions”. However, both natural and social sciences are necessary in building an effective solution that strives for ecological sustainability. This sharing presents the idea of common but differentiated local solutions towards solving the problem of global climate change, and also discusses other challenges: In what ways can we meaningfully engage an increasingly pluralistic society for collective environmental action? How do we create active allies for the environment out of passive ones, and even out of opposition actors? Navigating these social challenges can help progress sustainable development from the very baseline of any society, creating pathways to prosperity for both people and planet. Synopsis ›


The Plastic Project - Closing the Plastic Loop as a Community Ms. Amber Soong

Founder & Project Director,
The Plastic Project

Not many have had the opportunity to go underwater and experience a whole new world; and fewer have watched manta rays and other marine animals choke on bags of potato chips and diapers. Amber will talk about how plastic affects not only animals but also how it directly affects every single person as well. Underwater photos, The Plastic Project's beach clean ups in the mangroves and waterways of Singapore, and even trips to our wet markets will evidently show how much plastic is destroying our natural world. This talk will establish how one person's action can lead to a bigger movement. Synopsis ›

“CLIMATE ACTION: PITFALLS, PROSPECTS, AND THE WAY FORWARD”


Avoiding the Pitfalls of Fatalism, Polarisation and Greenwashing: One Perspective on Climate Conversations and Climate Action Mr. Jaime Ho

Partner,
Finsbury Glover Hering

This session explores the diverse and growing challenges in addressing and communicating climate action. From fatalism to fatigue, to greenwashing and unfulfilled promises, the challenges to meaningful action will likely sharpen together with the real day-to-day threats of climate change to communities around the world. How best to identify and avoid these pitfalls? Is it possible to create broad-based societal movements on climate action, which can address long-standing issues such as climate justice, while avoiding the larger trends of polarisation in political debate today? What role will the youth of today play? This session will hope to provoke discussions on the necessary next steps forward. Synopsis ›

“ENVIRONMENTAL CITIZENSHIP AND THE GEOGRAPHIES OF RESPONSIBILITY"


Dr. Aidan Wong

Assistant Professor of Humanities (Education),
College of Integrative Studies,
Singapore Management University

Debates surrounding climate change often involve an element of attributing blame for the state of the environment; often this blame is outward, with an (un)known “other” taking the fall for our declining condition. Several governments and NGOs have gone to great pains to emphasise the importance of individual responsibility for the environment, otherwise known as environmental or ecological citizenship. But how successful has this been in changing social behaviour and perceptions of responsibility? This presentation draws on some preliminary studies on social behaviour related to the management of electronic waste in Singapore, and posits that much more needs to be done to nudge people towards becoming active environmental citizens. MARINA BARRAGE

Marina Barrage will showcase the Sustainable Singapore Gallery. The gallery experience is a modern and interactive gallery that provides on overview of Singapore’s sustainable development. GARDENS BY THE BAY

Gardens by the Bay will feature a “Kingfisher Wetlands Augmented Reality Tour”. The Kingfisher Wetlands is a unique freshwater wetlands in the city centre, situated along the waterfront promenade of Gardens by the Bay, near the open-air food court Satay by the Bay. It was opened last year to promote an interest and appreciation among the wider community on the diversity of wildlife in Singapore. As part of Gardens by the Bay’s latest sustainability efforts, more than 200 native mangrove trees and mangrove associates were planted as part of a pilot study to understand the potential for urban wetlands in storing “blue” carbon. Participants will get to enhance their visitor experience in the Gardens and discover the biodiversity and sustainability features of the Gardens through a pilot Augmented Reality tour.

Jo Tang, Treehugger Ms. Joline Tang

Founder,
Sustainability Project

Being sustainable is not as easy as ABC kind of task in Singapore. Having been into sustainability for the past 11 years, the speaker has faced many rejections in her life and discouragement about sustainability. “You cannot save the earth.” “Why are you trying so hard?”. “It is for the next generation to worry about, not us.”These are phrases she encountered or you might have heard them yourself when you are trying your best to be sustainable and be in this low waste journey.
