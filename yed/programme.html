PANEL: TOWARDS SUSTAINABLE FUTURES: PUBLIC PRIVATE PARTNERSHIPS IN RENEWABLES REVOLUTION


DESIGNING FUTURE FOOD SYSTEMS: BLACK SOLDIER FLIES (BSF) & UPCYCLING FOOD WASTE IN THE CITY Niraly Mangal

Doctoral Researcher in Black Soldier Flies for Circular Urban Food System, Singapore-ETH Centre
Department of Humanities, Social and Political Sciences (DGESS), ETH Zurich

The small island state Singapore imports 90% of food from overseas and has less than 1% of land for agricultural use. Furthermore, since 2015, Singapore has generated more than 4 million tonnes of food waste and on average less than 20% of this waste has been recycled annually. A future-proof food system guided by innovative methods can be both land and resource efficient, and at the same time provide an alternate source to animal or plant-derived proteins. In this context, black soldier flies (BSF) represent an opportunity for realising a key principle of the circular economy by targeting organic waste for continuous circulation into novel forms of value. BSF are fast-growing insects that can convert the food waste locally into high quality animal feed and fertilizers which then can be used by other forms of urban agriculture such as vertical faming, indoor farming, aquaponics etc. Thus, we can diversify local food system by developing integrated approach for food waste management and sustainable food production using BSF in high density urban environments. PANEL: REGIONAL OUTLOOK: TRANSBOUNDARY NARRATIVES IN SOUTHEAST ASIA'S CLIMATE CRISIS


NDCS IN ASEAN COUNTRIES: DREAMS OR REALITY? CASE STUDIES: INDONESIA AND THE PHILIPPINES A/P Linda Sulistiawati

NUS Law, IPCC

The Asia Pacific region has contributed approximately 83% to the global emission growth since 2010. Among the surge of emission in the Asia Pacific region, the ASEAN countries has been noted to have the highest growth rate – even globally. The region’s trends and drivers are dominated by energy and AFOLU sectors. At the same time having significant emission growth, ASEAN Countries are among the most vulnerable and prone to be affected by climate change impacts. From such description, it can be said that ASEAN countries have the same interest in combatting climate change. As of 2017, all ASEAN countries had signed and ratified the Paris Agreement along with submitting their NDCs. Presently, most of the ASEAN members, have submitted their enhanced NDCs. Although enhanced NDCs displays progresses in national climate action, the pledges will never be effective unless they are actually implemented in the Country level. This research tries to depict regulating efforts to reach those NDC targets in the sub-national level of ASEAN countries, through examining the local government efforts in dealing with climate change issue and fulfilling their NDC targets. Synopsis ›

THE VOLUNTARY CARBON CREDIT MARKET – CHALLENGES, OPPORTUNITIES AND TRANSNATIONAL FLOWS Tun Shien Foo

Origination & Technical Analyst, Climate Impact X

The voluntary carbon market is where entities purchase carbon credits to offset a portion or all of their carbon emissions. The carbon credits are issued in relation to projects that mitigate the impacts of climate change. The voluntary carbon market has grown in recent years but still has a greater potential for helping to mitigate climate change. The voluntary carbon market brings together global stakeholders where their actions, including the development of projects or purchase of credits, result in impacts not limited to national boundaries.

The presentation will provide an overview of the mechanics of the voluntary carbon market and how activities in the market are transnational." Synopsis ›

IS GREEN REALLY GREEN? Darren Webb

Evolution Data Centre

Data centres are often referred to as the lungs of the digital infrastructure ecosystem, they keep the data pumping around the world. In today’s world they are critical to our very being (if you don’t believe try not using the internet or your phone for 24 hours). Yet they consumer huge amounts of power, and can take up considerable construction materials, land and other valuable resources, such as water (to cool the servers). The industry, and the customers it supports, wants them to become “green”, however are there different shades of green, or can green ever really be green? Synopsis ›

RE-IMAGINING THE HUMAN DIMENSION OF SUSTAINABLE ENERGY TRANSITIONS IN SOUTHEAST ASIA Mary Ann Franco

Senior Consultant, Aquatera

Developing countries in Southeast Asia addresses the lack of energy access and security by implementing policies that encourage renewable energy technologies (RE). For the off-grid and remote areas, the government prioritises rural electrification through solar home systems or electricity subsidies. However, even with national targets, the implementation appears more challenging, especially at the community level. Scholars and practitioners argue that the communities' awareness, engagement, and participation are important factors in effectively achieving clean energy transition. My presentation examines the impact of the community participation on the success (or failure) of long-term RET adoption in poor rural communities. The key question is: how could we RE-imagine the human dimension of RE futures in Southeast Asia? Synopsis ›

FOOD WASTE SOLUTIONS IN THE TECH ERA: FEEDING US WITH HOPE OR FAT HOPE? Preston Wong

CEO & Co-Founder, treatsure

Food waste has been a particularly sticky problem for countries to resolve. In Singapore, food waste levels remain high in 2023 compared to 2021, with a near 10% increase from pre-pandemic levels. Various food waste solutions have emerged over the years with advancements in technology. However, do they really help us to achieve zero food waste, and might they actually bring more harm to stakeholders than intended? Drawing from his experiences in the food waste and sustainability space, Mr Preston Wong will discuss the opportunities and tradeoffs that current food waste solutions present towards the climate crisis.
